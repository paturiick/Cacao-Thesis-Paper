\chapter{METHODOLOGY}

This chapter presents the methodology of the study. It describes the research design, setting, data-gathering procedures, and the design and implementation of the system to ensure that the study was conducted systematically and aligned with its objectives.

\section{Research Design}
This study adopted the Modified Waterfall Model of the Systems Development Life Cycle (SDLC), shown in figure~\ref{fig:waterfall}, as its methodology.

The methodology was selected due to its structured yet flexible nature, allowing for sequential phases with opportunities for feedback and refinement. For this study, it enabled the researchers to systematically design, implement, and evaluate the integration of UAV-collected imagery with YOLO for early disease detection in cacao pods, ensuring that each phase was thoroughly reviewed before progressing to the next, while still accommodating necessary adjustments. 

\section{Research Setting}

The study was conducted at the Janog Cacao Plantation located in Initao, Misamis Oriental, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:cacao_farm}.

The plantation covers an area of approximately five (5) hectares and contains around 2,500 cacao trees. Each tree has an average height of 2 to 2.5 meters and is planted at 3-meter intervals, forming organized rows that are suitable for structured UAV flights. The site represents a typical small to medium-scale cacao farm in the region and is relevant to the Philippine cacao industry, which continues to face challenges from black pod disease.

Local farmers served as end-users, providing feedback on the practicality of UAV operations and the usability of the detection system. The plantation’s accessibility to the research team, along with its realistic farming conditions where advanced monitoring systems are rarely implemented, made it an ideal setting for developing and testing the system.


\section{Research Setup}

The research setup utilized a UAV (Unmanned Aerial Vehicle) equipped with a built-in high-definition camera and GPS module to conduct low-altitude monitoring of the cacao farm. The UAV operated at approximately 1 meter above ground level to capture detailed visual information of cacao pods under the canopy, where early symptoms of black pod rot were more visible than in high-altitude passes. The UAV followed predefined flight paths along the corridors between cacao rows to ensure systematic coverage of the plantation, utilizing the existing farm layout to define the traversal trajectory.

\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\caption{UAV Flight Paths Across the Cacao Farm}
	\label{fig:uav_flight_paths}
	\includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{figures/Uav_Flight_Paths.pdf}

Figure~\ref{fig:uav_flight_paths} presents the UAV’s flight paths across the cacao farm, illustrating the specific orientation strategies used to minimize blind spots. While the UAV moved parallel to each cacao row, it maintained a yaw (heading) orientation such that the camera faced the cacao trees during recording. This side-facing strategy kept pods consistently inside the camera field of view and reduced missed captures caused by limited viewpoints. To address pod occlusion caused by leaves and branches, the UAV implemented multi-view coverage by alternating its facing direction across passes (e.g., right-facing on one pass and left-facing on the return pass). Smooth turns at row ends (U-turns) were performed to transition to the next row segment, ensuring stable real-time YOLO inference for detecting visible symptoms of \textit{Phytophthora palmivora}.

\section{Data Gathering}
To ensure a well-rounded and effective system design, data for this study were collected from various relevant sources:

\subsection*{Sources of Data}
\textbf{Cacao Farmers and Field Personnel}. Surveys and interviews were conducted with cacao growers and farm workers to gather insights on existing practices for disease detection, issues encountered in the field, and expectations for a UAV-based system. 

\textbf{Agricultural Specialists}. Input from agricultural professionals was obtained to identify key disease symptoms, validate detection criteria, and provide guidance on monitoring strategies for cacao pod health.

\textbf{Existing Literature and Research Studies}. A review of literature, including studies by Baculio \& Barbosa (2022), Vera et al. (2024), and Solpot (2020), supported system design by providing benchmarks on accuracy, image processing, and machine learning in agriculture.

\textbf{Technology Experts}. Consultations with UAV technicians, AI developers, and computer vision experts were sought to ensure the system’s technological components such as image acquisition and model training were both feasible and optimized for agricultural environments.

\subsection*{Data Gathering Procedure}

To gather relevant data that informed the design and development of the cacao pod disease detection system, this study utilized both questionnaires and interviews.

\textbf{Questionnaire}. A structured questionnaire was distributed to cacao farmers and field personnel to gather information about their current practices in detecting cacao pod diseases, the challenges they faced in early identification, and their perspectives on using UAV-based solutions. Before distribution, the questionnaire was reviewed and approved by the research adviser, agricultural specialists, and academic authorities to ensure that it was technically sound, ethically appropriate, and aligned with the objectives of the study.

\textbf{Interview}. Interviews were conducted with cacao farmers, agricultural experts, plant pathologists, UAV technicians, and AI developers. These interviews aimed to collect in-depth insights on disease symptoms, detection indicators, drone imaging strategies, and technical requirements for integrating YOLO into an agricultural setting.

\textbf{Existing Literature}. A review of relevant literature was conducted to understand the current state of disease detection systems in agriculture, particularly focusing on cacao pod diseases. This included studies on the use of UAVs, AI-driven disease detection models (such as YOLO), and the challenges associated with deploying such technologies in farming environments and provided a foundation for comparing the system with existing solutions.

\subsection*{Data Finding Analysis}

To gather relevant data, this study employed multiple data collection methods.

\textbf{Qualitative Analysis}. Thematic analysis was applied to interviews and open-ended survey responses. This approach helped identify recurring themes such as difficulties in manual disease detection, trust in machine learning techniques, and challenges related to the adoption of UAVs. Insights from this analysis informed user-centered system design and guided improvements in usability and functionality.

\textbf{Quantitative Analysis}. Descriptive statistical analysis was used for the structured survey data. This included calculating frequencies, percentages, and average values to measure levels of technological readiness, prevalence of black pod disease, and the willingness of users to adopt UAV-based solutions for monitoring. These metrics provided measurable indicators to support system feature prioritization.


\textbf{Feasibility Analysis}. Sensor specifications and machine learning model performance were assessed through expert consultations and a review of relevant literature. This included evaluating the accuracy of YOLO for disease detection and the practicality of drone operation in cacao farm environments using a performance matrix.

\textbf{Spatial Analysis}. Given that the UAV system captured geo-tagged images, spatial analysis was conducted to examine the geographical distribution of detected disease cases. This analysis helped visualize infection hotspots across the farm and supported precision intervention strategies. Tools such as heatmaps or geospatial clustering were used to map and interpret disease spread over time and space.

\section{Requirement Gathering}

This section presents the requirements gathering conducted by the researchers. The data were collected through an interview with a local cacao farmer who has extensive experience in cacao production and management. The purpose of this interview was to understand the existing farming practices, challenges in disease detection and management, and collect essential information for system development. 

Before the engagement, the researchers prepared a set of open- and closed-ended questions to guide the discussion. A consent letter outlining the ethical use of the collected information was also presented to the respondent, ensuring that all gathered data would be used solely for academic and research purposes in compliance with data privacy regulations. (Refer to Appendix A) 

The respondent has been cultivating cacao for nine (9) years and manages a farm covering approximately five (5) hectares with more than 2,500 cacao trees planted at a spacing of three (3) meters per tree. The farm contains various elements, including tall coconut trees that provide shade, nearby power lines, and a river, none of which significantly affect the farm’s operations. The farmer personally inspects the cacao pods on foot, as this is currently the only available method to monitor the trees for pest infestations and diseases.

According to the respondent, major challenges in cacao farming include high costs and labor-intensive practices, particularly in spraying, pruning, and pest control. However, the most critical issue arises during the rainy season, as it contributes to the spread of Phytophthora, the fungus responsible for black pod rot, locally referred to as butikol. During these periods, only about one (1) out of every ten (10) trees remains healthy, and the number of harvestable pods varies significantly depending on weather conditions.

The farmer identifies diseased cacao pods primarily through their appearance, observing signs such as discoloration and the presence of small dark dots, which are associated with Phytophthora infection. Once symptoms appear, even at an early stage, the affected pods are immediately removed to prevent further contamination of nearby fruits. Although spraying is conducted as a preventive measure, some infected pods remain unnoticed due to the vast area of the farm and the intensity of labor required.

Farm inspection is done daily, taking approximately three (3) weeks to complete a full cycle across all areas of the farm. The farmer increases inspection frequency during the rainy season, as losses are significantly higher compared to the dry season. Fertilizers are also applied for quality control, with an estimated expenditure of thirty thousand pesos (₱30,000) per cycle.

During the peak harvest season, typically from November to December, the farm produces around 300 to 500 kilograms of cacao beans, which are sold to vendors from Davao at a price of ₱300 per kilogram. However, yields lower than 200 kilograms are considered a deficit. Based on the respondent’s estimate, approximately twenty (20) pods are required to produce one (1) kilogram of beans. The pods vary in color red to orange, and green to yellow with yellow pods indicating ripeness. Harvesting and pruning are performed manually using pruners or saws, with extra care taken to avoid damaging the fruit-bearing branches. (see Appendix B)

\subsection{System Architecture}

Figure~\ref{fig:SysArch} illustrates the system architecture of the study.

The architecture was designed as a closed-loop system centered around the cacao tree, which served as the source of input data. The drone (DJI RoboMaster TT) acted as the primary data acquisition unit, capturing visual input from the trees. To ensure spatial accuracy, an external GPS NEO-M8N module was integrated into the drone's expansion board; this module continuously transmitted geolocation data to the drone, allowing for the synchronization of images with specific coordinates.

Data transmission was facilitated through a wireless Wi-Fi network. A high-gain Wi-Fi adapter connected to the ground station established a bidirectional link, enabling the system to send flight commands to the drone and retrieve telemetry and video data in real-time. The ground station functioned as the central controller and processing hub, where the farmer interacted with the system. The farmer inputted mission parameters ("send data") and received actionable insights ("output data") regarding the health status of the crops. Finally, a database was integrated with the ground station to permanently store mission logs and detection results, allowing for the retrieval of historical data for long-term analysis.

The Ground Station served as the central control and processing hub of the system, responsible for handling all backend operations, data management, and user interaction. Essentially, the Ground Station ensured that raw UAV data was transformed into actionable information, facilitating efficient monitoring, analysis, and decision-making for cacao pod disease detection in real time. It was composed of two closely connected parts: the web application and the server components.

The web application, developed in Vue.js, served as the main user interface, providing modules for authentication, drone mission control, and visualization of detection outputs. Django submitted the images to the YOLO detection module and then attached geospatial information derived from the UAV’s GPS data. These enriched results were stored in PostgreSQL and subsequently retrieved by the web application for display. QGIS also retrieved this geospatial data directly from PostgreSQL to produce interactive maps and conduct further spatial assessments of the detected crop conditions.

\subsection{YOLOv11-Based Detection Implementation}
YOLO (You Only Look Once) is a CNN-based object detection framework that employs Convolutional Neural Networks to simultaneously determine the location of objects within an image and classify them. Unlike traditional image classification models, which merely indicate the presence of an object, YOLO predicts both what the object is and where it is situated by drawing bounding boxes around it in a single processing step.

YOLOv11 was employed since it introduced architectural improvements in feature extraction, particularly for small-object detection, and achieved higher accuracy with fewer parameters compared to earlier versions. YOLOv11 also provided scalable model variants that allowed the researchers to balance accuracy and computational requirements, making it highly suited for UAV-captured aerial imagery where cacao pods appeared small, numerous, and partially obscured.

Figure~\ref{fig:yolov11_architecture} shows the backbone for feature extraction, the neck for aggregating multi-scale feature maps, and the detection head outputs used for classifying and locating objects at various scales.

\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\caption{Architecture of YOLOv11, showing backbone with new C3K2 blocks, attention modules (C2PSA), Spatial Pyramid Pooling Fast (SPFF), and multi-scale detection heads (adapted from Khanam and Hussain, 2024)}
	\label{fig:yolov11_architecture}

The Intersection over Union (IoU) metric was used to evaluate the model’s ability to detect diseased cacao pods by measuring the overlap between predicted and ground-truth bounding boxes. It was chosen because IoU is a widely accepted standard for assessing YOLO-based object detection tasks. While it measured the overlap, combining it with precision, recall, and mean Average Precision (mAP) provided better testing metrics for the model performance.

\subsubsection*{Dataset Collection Method}

The dataset for this study consisted solely of a custom image dataset captured using a DJI RoboMaster TT’s built-in camera during scheduled flights over cacao plantations. Images were collected at multiple viewing angles and flight altitudes to capture variations in pod orientation, scale, and perspective. To improve robustness under real field conditions, the dataset intentionally included healthy and diseased cacao pods across diverse illumination levels (e.g., shaded canopy vs direct sunlight), weather-related appearance changes, and natural background clutter. Particular focus was placed on documenting multiple stages of infection and varying degrees of pod occlusion by leaves, branches, or neighboring pods. All captured images were manually annotated to label the regions of interest, providing the ground-truth required for supervised training of the YOLO model.

\subsubsection*{Data Preprocessing}

As shown in Figure \ref{fig:Data Preprocessing Workflow}, the process began with raw imagery collected through Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) and external datasets. The UAV-captured videos were converted into individual frames at fixed intervals, ensuring that every pod instance was represented across varying viewpoints. Blurry, redundant, or low-quality frames were systematically removed to eliminate noise and improve dataset consistency. The selected images were resized and normalized according to YOLOv11’s input dimensions (e.g., 640×640 pixels), preserving the aspect ratio to prevent geometric distortion. Normalization scaled pixel values into a uniform range (typically 0–1) to enhance computational efficiency and model stability.

\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering

	\label{fig:Data Preprocessing Workflow}

The curated dataset was then divided into training, validation, and testing subsets, following a 70–20–10 ratio. This split ensured balanced class representation and prevented overfitting by maintaining a consistent ratio of healthy and diseased pods across subsets. Data augmentation was applied exclusively to the training set to simulate real-world variability and improve the model’s generalization. Transformations such as random horizontal and vertical flips, rotations, brightness and contrast adjustments, and cropping exposed the model to different illumination, orientation, and scale conditions. All annotations were standardized into the YOLO label format, which specified object class IDs and bounding box coordinates in normalized values. This standardized structure ensured seamless integration with YOLOv11’s training and detection pipeline, enabling the model to correctly interpret positional and categorical information during learning.


\subsubsection*{Cacao and Disease Detection}

The UAV-captured cacao images were fed into the YOLOv11 model for automated object detection and disease classification. YOLOv11 divided each image into a predefined grid structure, where each grid cell predicted multiple bounding boxes, corresponding confidence scores, and class probabilities. This design allowed the model to simultaneously identify multiple cacao pods within a single frame, efficiently localizing each pod even under challenging conditions such as dense foliage, overlapping objects, or inconsistent lighting. Detected pods were enclosed within bounding boxes that represented the system’s prediction of their exact position within the image.

Once localization was complete, YOLOv11 proceeded with the classification of each detected pod as either healthy or infected with black pod disease. This classification was based on visual patterns learned during model training, including texture irregularities, surface discoloration, dark lesions, and other symptoms characteristic of infection. The model leveraged deep feature extraction through its backbone network, multiscale feature fusion in the neck, and final detection through its head architecture. The output consisted of annotated images displaying bounding boxes and class labels, where healthy pods were distinctly marked from diseased ones. This end-to-end workflow enabled precise, efficient, and scalable cacao pod monitoring, facilitating early detection and management of black pod disease in agricultural environments.

\subsubsection*{Real-Time Processing, Detection, and Result Output}
Real-time processing and detection were performed during UAV flight by executing the detection pipeline on the ground station immediately as frames were received from the live video stream. Each incoming frame underwent lightweight, per-frame standardization before inference, including color format conversion, letterbox resizing to the model input size, and pixel normalization. The standardized frame was then forwarded to YOLOv11 to generate detections consisting of bounding boxes, class labels, and confidence scores. Detection results were displayed immediately through on-screen overlays on the ground station for monitoring and verification, and detection logs were written concurrently with timestamps and available location metadata to support live reporting. Under this workflow, continuous video was not archived; instead, only detection outputs and selected evidence frames were retained to minimize storage requirements while preserving traceability of reported detections.

\subsubsection*{Object Tracking and Duplicate Suppression}
To avoid counting the same cacao pod multiple times across consecutive frames, an object tracking mechanism was integrated using ByteTrack. For each incoming frame, YOLOv11 produced bounding boxes, class labels, and confidence scores. These detections were then forwarded to ByteTrack, which associated detections across frames and assigned a persistent track ID per pod. A pod was recorded as a unique instance only once per track ID within a defined time window, while subsequent detections of the same track were treated as the same pod.

To further reduce duplicate records under rapid camera motion or unstable tracking, an Intersection-over-Union (IoU) consistency check was applied between consecutive bounding boxes of the same track ID. When the IoU fell below a defined threshold, the detection was treated as a movement/instability event and required additional confirmation across subsequent frames before being logged as a valid record. This combined tracking and IoU-based consistency filtering reduced duplicate logs caused by camera motion, overlapping views, or repeated observation of the same pod during flight, improving the reliability of pod counts and geo-tagged reports.


\subsubsection*{Visual and Object Geo-localization}

The UAV was equipped with a GPS module and a forward-facing camera that captured images of the cacao pods during flight. Each image was associated with the DJI RoboMaster TT’s GPS at the moment of capture. The images were then processed using an object detection model (YOLOv11), which generated bounding boxes around identified cacao pods. A bounding box was defined by its pixel coordinates $(x, y, w, h)$, where $(x, y)$ is the top-left corner, and $w$ and $h$ are the width and height in pixels.

To estimate whether the drone's GPS could be used as an approximate location for a cacao pod, the researchers used the size of the pod's bounding box in the image together with the camera's field of view and image resolution. This approach followed the methodology of \cite{leorna2022estimating}, which utilized the pinhole camera model to relate an object's real-world width, its distance from the camera, and its size in pixels. First, the camera's focal length in pixels was computed using the formula:

\begin{equation}
	f = \frac{\text{image width in pixels}}{2\tan(\text{HFOV}/2)},
\end{equation}
\myequation{Computation of focal length}

where $HFOV$ is the horizontal field of view in radians. The DJI RoboMaster TT has a 5 MP image (2592 pixels wide) with a horizontal FoV of $82.6^\circ$. This gave a focal length of approximately 2620.45 pixels. The calculated pixel-width threshold for the cacao pod was:

\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:pixel_width_threshold_cacao_pod}
	P_{\text{th}} = \frac{W \cdot f}{D_{\max}},
\end{equation}
\myequation{Pixel-width threshold for cacao pod}

where $W$ is the real width of the pod (e.g., 0.10 m) and $D_{\max}$ is the maximum distance from the drone at which the researchers were willing to accept its GPS as an approximate pod location (e.g., 1.0 m). Using these numbers, the threshold was approximately 262.045 pixels, calculated from eq. (\ref{eq:pixel_width_threshold_cacao_pod}) \citep{leorna2022estimating}. This meant that any cacao pod whose bounding box width was equal to or greater than 262.045 pixels was considered close enough that the drone's GPS could be used as an approximate pod location, while smaller bounding boxes corresponded to pods that were farther away and whose GPS positions would have been inaccurate.

As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:tree}, the pod has a bounding box with 300 pixels; widths greater than the threshold (262.045 pixels) were close enough that the drone GPS could be used as an approximate pod location, while smaller boxes corresponded to pods that were farther away.

Figure \ref{fig:robomaster_tt} illustrates the DJI RoboMaster TT. It was chosen for its lightweight design, stability at low altitudes, and compatibility with open-source programming platforms.

The RoboMaster TT integrated several important functions. Its flight system combined a flight controller and propulsion motors with the Vision Positioning System (VPS), which utilized a downward-facing camera and infrared sensors to maintain stable hovering at low altitudes without relying on GPS. This feature was essential for operating close to the ground within cacao plantations.

The onboard 5 MP camera enabled the capture of still images and 720p HD video, suitable for documenting cacao pods. Power was supplied by a 3.8 V, 1100 mAh LiPo battery, which supported flight times of up to 13 minutes in the initial specifications. Safety features included propeller guards, battery protection, and automatic landing in case of weak signals or low power. The expansion kit included an ESP32-based open-source controller, providing UART, I2C, GPIO, PWM, and SPI interfaces for integrating additional modules such as the GPS receiver.

Data communication followed a cross-coupled configuration where the GPS TX (Transmit) pin connected to the ESP32 RX (Receive) pin to stream NMEA data containing geospatial coordinates. This direct UART connection enabled the ESP32 to parse geospatial data with low latency, which was then synchronized with the video feed for the telemetry overlay.

\subsection*{Command-based Mission Control with RoboMaster TT SDK}
The Software Development Kit (SDK) enabled command-based mission planning with the DJI RoboMaster TT. Through the SDK, the drone executed scripted flight commands such as \texttt{takeoff}, \texttt{forward(x)}, \texttt{back(x)}, \texttt{cw(angle)}, and \texttt{land}, allowing it to follow predefined flight paths across cacao rows. During these missions, the drone captured images of cacao pods at specific intervals, paired with location data provided by the integrated NEO-M8N GPS module.

An offline detection approach was adopted: the collected images and GPS coordinates were stored and later processed using the YOLO algorithm. The results of detection were then mapped in QGIS to identify and visualize areas affected by \textit{Phytophthora palmivora}. This workflow synchronized drone navigation, image acquisition, and geotagging while accounting for the hardware limitations of the RoboMaster TT.

\subsection*{GPS Integration and Data Handling}
A NEO-M8N GPS module was connected to the open-source controller via UART for geolocation. The NEO-M8N is a high-performance GNSS receiver capable of tracking multiple satellite systems (GPS, GLONASS, Galileo, and BeiDou). It provided efficient position, velocity, and time data, which were synchronized with each captured image to create geotagged datasets. This integration enabled the precise mapping of cacao pods and potential disease symptoms in QGIS.

All communications between the UAV and the ground station were established through a Wi-Fi connection. The SDK relied on UDP packets transmitted over Wi-Fi to send flight commands and receive status updates. For structured telemetry such as GPS coordinates and mission logs, the MQTT protocol was integrated as a lightweight messaging layer, ensuring reliable delivery of small data packets. Image files, due to their larger size, were transferred directly via the Wi-Fi link to the ground station.

After each mission, the drone transmitted images and GPS logs to a central database. The ground station retrieved these records from the database for preprocessing and analysis. This workflow ensured that raw images and geospatial metadata were securely stored and easily accessible for the YOLO-based detection system and subsequent spatial visualization in QGIS.

\subsubsection*{Telemetry Overlay}

During flight, the UAV continuously recorded telemetry data consisting of timestamp, latitude, longitude, altitude, and orientation parameters (yaw, pitch, and roll). These telemetry values were synchronized with the video stream and image frames, ensuring that every recorded frame had an associated spatiotemporal context for efficient tracking.

Telemetry overlay, as shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:telemetry_overlay}, refers to the process of superimposing flight data directly onto the visual feed or processed image output.

\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\caption{Telemetry Overlay}
	\label{fig:telemetry_overlay}
	\includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{figures/Telemetry.pdf}
\end{figure}

This provided operators with real-time situational awareness and enabled more precise post-flight analysis. The telemetry overlay displayed the UAV’s recording time, positional coordinates, altitude, and orientation alongside the YOLOv11 bounding boxes of detected cacao trees. It helped users quickly interpret both detection and flight information together. This integration ensured that detections were not only localized within an image but also connected to the UAV’s position and state during capture.

The inclusion of telemetry information served multiple functions:
(1) The timestamp allowed for chronological indexing of detections, supporting analysis of cacao disease monitoring.
(2) The geographic coordinates (latitude, longitude, altitude) provided a direct spatial reference to the UAV’s position during detection.
(3) The IMU-derived orientation (yaw, pitch, roll) supplied critical context on the UAV’s attitude, which could affect the angle and scale of captured images.

Together, these parameters created a transparent record of the flight path and its associated detections, enabling efficient mapping, post-flight analysis, and data validation.

\section{Test and Evaluation}

Testing was conducted to ensure that the system operated efficiently and was suitable for real-world use in cacao farm management. The process involved functional testing, usability testing, and performance testing. Functional testing verified whether key features such as UAV image capture, detection of Phytophthora palmivora-infected cacao pods using YOLO, and geotagging of infected trees performed as intended, with each function tested through defined inputs and expected outputs. Usability testing assessed how easily end users, particularly cacao farmers, navigated and interacted with the system by performing essential tasks such as logging in, accessing detection results, and interpreting geotagged maps. Feedback was collected using the System Usability Scale (SUS) to evaluate the overall user experience. Performance testing measured the system’s responsiveness and accuracy, focusing on the time taken from image capture to the display of results, as well as the efficiency in processing high-resolution images and managing data. Together, these tests validated the system’s reliability, user-friendliness, and effectiveness in supporting early disease detection and timely intervention.

\subsection{Functional Testing}

Functional testing was carried out to ensure that every part of the system worked as intended. This testing began once the prototype was complete. It focused on verifying key features such as capturing images through the drone, detecting healthy and infected cacao pods using YOLO, tagging infected trees’ locations with GPS, and displaying results clearly on the dashboard. Each function was tested by providing specific inputs and checking if the outputs matched the expected results.

\subsection{Usability Testing}

Usability testing was conducted to make sure the system was easy and practical for cacao farmers to use. After the prototype was ready, farmers were invited to try important features such as logging in, flying the drone, viewing pod detection results, and checking the map showing infected trees. After using the system, they filled out a survey called the System Usability Scale (SUS), which measured how user-friendly the system felt. The survey used a rating scale from 1 to 5 and asked about how easy the system was to learn, how confident they felt using it, and whether the features worked well together. Scores were converted to a total out of 100, where scores above 68 generally indicate that a system is easy to use. This process helped the team understand what worked well and what needed improvement before the system was fully deployed.

\pagebreak

\subsection*{Performance Testing}

Performance testing was conducted to evaluate the system’s responsiveness, accuracy, and overall reliability based on its core functionalities. This included measuring the accuracy of detecting Phytophthora palmivora infected cacao pods using YOLO, assessing the precision of geolocation through the GPS module and QGIS, and recording the system’s response time from image capture to the display of results on the dashboard. The testing also evaluated the system using standard performance metrics such as Accuracy, Precision, Recall, F1-Score, and Error Rate to determine the effectiveness of the detection model. These evaluations verified whether the system met its intended performance criteria and was capable of supporting timely and informed decision-making in cacao disease management.
